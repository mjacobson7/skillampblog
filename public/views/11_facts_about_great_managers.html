11 Facts About Great Managers

Nothing is beneath a great manager who is looking out for his or her team.

Posted by Ryan Gray on April 12, 2018

There is no secret or hack to being a great manager. It takes time, effort and sincerity. I learned this lesson the hard way.

When I first became a supervisor, I was only managing one employee. But, after seven months of massive growth, my team grew to five people. As the team continued to grow, I read every blog post I could find about management. I tried to find out what structure or management style worked best. In the end, I found there is not one secret answer to excellent management. Instead, there are small essential things you must do to be a great manager and get the most out of your team.

Below I will include a list of things I have found work great to create a stable team. There is no particular order to any of the points listed below. To avoid being overwhelmed, I recommend you pick a few to focus on for a few weeks and add on as you continue to grow. Remember excellent management does not happen all at once! It takes time.

  1. Hold one-on-ones weekly with each employee. To read on how to conduct an effective one-on-one, read our other post here.

  2. Use "we" instead of "I" when speaking to and about your team. Great managers think of everyone as a team. It is not what I am doing; it's what we as a team are doing.

  3. When things go well, your team did it, and they deserve all the credit. When things go poorly, it is your fault, and you deserve all the blame.

  4. Have a budget for you and your team to do an activity once a month during the work day (you heard me). I truly believe it is essential to take a break from work and do something fun together. This could be a movie, dinner at a nice restaurant, laser tag, etc. Show your team they matter. Do something fun monthly.

  5. Everyone on your team should have a bonus structure regardless of position. Sales positions should always have the highest bonus. The harder the job and more important to revenue, the higher the reward.

  6. Your own personal bonus structure should align perfectly with your team's goals and bonuses. If your team succeeds, you succeed. If your team fails, you fail.

  7. Admit when you are wrong. Do not hide this from your team.

  8. Praise in public and criticize in private. Let everyone know what you admire about the members of your team. Additionally, no one in your organization should ever be humiliated in front of everyone else. Respect their dignity.

  9. It is your job to set the expectations (goals) for your team, and it is the job of your team members to accomplish them. If they exceed the goals for the quarter, reward them for it.

  10. The job of a manager is to remove all obstacles that impede his or her team from accomplishing their goals. If that means taking specific calls or doing particular menial tasks, then so be it. Nothing is beneath a great manager who is looking out for his or her team.
